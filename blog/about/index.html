  The process is not linear and it depends on the project itself.

  Here you are able to define the needed resources. These resources can be stakeholders, research methods, research tools (such as hotjar), inspirations, competitors. Also to define the projects and the goals in your own words.

 

 
ü§ì Write the problem statement - analytical, hypothetical, technical, design problem findings.


 

  This depends on the nature of the project is the project is a new product or a product with some problems. The main point here is the perfect understanding of the project and well planning and testing a hypothesis.

 

 
ü§î User research.


 

  Selecting and identifying the main users and the target of your product and setting a meeting with them. Research for your users and create a persona. Writing the interview questions, trying to formulate the gaps that you want to fill into open-ended questions

 

 
ü§ù Recruit and interview users.


 

  By now I should have enough material to recruit the users and ask them questions. I try to be curious as much as I can. The more I learn about your product/project, the more curious and knowledgeable of the information that I want to dig out of the users.

 

 
üé® Design and iterate.


 

  I always begin by writing down and sketching the objectives and what needs to be on each screen before opening Figma and making beautiful magic. I believe that a UX designer's creativity killer is Figma. When you begin designing on Figma, you easily get caught up in colors and pixels and general UI instead of UX.

 

 
üìã Testing and getting feedback.


 

  Post-release research is important to know how are users using the product post-release. And of course, you may need to get back to some steps in the process again.
